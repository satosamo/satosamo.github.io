We are Bayesian statisticians.

We imagine a world where we know it may rain but it doesn't have to. We also know the grass may get wet but it doesn't have to.

We think nothing else can happen. Everything except raining and grass getting wet has a zero probability of happening.

Outcomes:

  • Let Pr(a)Pr(a)Pr(a) be the probability of outcome aaa called: it rains and the grass is wet.
  • Let Pr(b)Pr(b)Pr(b) be the probability of outcome bbb called: it rains and the grass is not wet.
  • Let Pr(c)Pr(c)Pr(c) be the probability of outcome ccc called: it doesn't rain and the grass is wet.
  • Let Pr(d)Pr(d)Pr(d) be the probability of outcome ddd called: it doesn't rains and the grass is not wet.

Events:

  • Event called: it rains - a subset Erain=(a,b)E_{\text{rain}} = (a, b)Erain​=(a,b)
  • Event called: it doesn't rain - a subset Eno rain=(c,d)E_{\text{no rain}} = (c, d)Eno rain​=(c,d)
  • Event called: the grass is wet - a subset Egrass wet=(a,c)E_{\text{grass wet}} = (a, c)Egrass wet​=(a,c)
  • Event called: the grass is not wet - a subset Egrass not wet=(b,d)E_{\text{grass not wet}} =(b, d)Egrass not wet​=(b,d)
  • Plus the four above

We are tasked with defining this:

"what is the probability that the grass is wet if it is given that it rains"


It would be sensible to ask this question: what is the difference between the 'probability that the grass is wet and that it rains' and the 'probability that the grass is wet given it rains'?

The first case is asking what is the probability of the intersection of events 'it rains' and 'grass is wet' which is the outcome AAA with probability P(a)P(a)P(a).

The second case is asking what is the probability of an event 'grass is wet' when any other outcome can come only from the event 'it rains'. Meaning, when fixing any event trough the statement: 'it is given that...', we create a new space of all possible outcomes - those that can occur only in the event we are taken as given.

In our case, giving 'it rains', we limit ourselves only to outcomes in event ErainsE_{rains}Erains​: (a,b)(a, b)(a,b).

We, however, did not touch the function or parameters of the world that determine the values of probabilities. This means, if no other outcome is possible, we would get P(a)+P(b)≠1P(a)+P(b)\neq 1P(a)+P(b)=1. The probabilities of P(a)P(a)P(a) and P(b)P(b)P(b) must thus be recalculated as those are the only two outcomes in our new universe of outcomes. Let the probability be P′P'P′.

The relation ship between the remaining outcomes has not changed. If one was twice as probable as the other one, that ratio must be conserved.
